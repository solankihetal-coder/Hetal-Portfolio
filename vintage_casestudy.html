The Story

"Style is a way to express your personality without having to speak."

This was my very first design project — submitted for a college competition with no prior professional experience, no design mentor, and no formal training in UI. Just a brief, a blank Figma canvas, and a strong instinct for what felt right aesthetically.

The brief was open-ended: design a landing page for a fashion brand. I chose to build Vintage Vogue — a brand concept rooted in timelessness, earthy elegance, and the quiet confidence of vintage fashion. The entire colour palette, layout, typography, and content were decided independently within the competition timeframe.

Vintage Vogue is a fictional fashion e-commerce brand designed for the modern woman who appreciates the quiet luxury of classic silhouettes. The landing page was designed to communicate brand identity, showcase collections, display social proof, and guide users toward a purchase — all within a single scrollable page.

The design spans 6 distinct sections: a hero, a new collection grid, a brand story panel, a best-seller product grid, a testimonials strip, and a footer.

03

Design Decisions

🏺

Earthy, Muted Palette

Beige, sand, khaki, and dark bark tones were chosen to evoke vintage fabric swatches — warm, tactile, and timeless. No bright accent colours; the palette itself is the brand identity.

✍️

Italic Serif for Headlines

Italic serif typography communicates fashion editorial energy — the same language used by Vogue, Elle, and Harper's Bazaar. It signals sophistication without a single extra design element.

📐

Asymmetric Hero Layout

The hero places the headline on the left with a large image placeholder on the right — a deliberate editorial asymmetry that feels curated, not template-like.

🃏

Rounded Category Cards

The collection cards use generous border-radius at only one corner — top-right — giving them an organic, unexpected shape that breaks the grid monotony.

📊

Stats Strip for Trust

A horizontal stats bar showing "Since 2015 · 9800+ Product Sales · 5200+ Best Rewards" establishes credibility and grounds the brand story section.

🔲

Dark Footer with Nav Repeat

Repeating the navigation inside the footer is a UX convention that reduces friction for users who've scrolled to the bottom and want to navigate without scrolling back up.

Why this combination works

The italic serif creates the editorial, high-fashion voice while a clean light sans-serif underneath keeps the UI functional and scannable. The contrast between formal and minimal is exactly the tension that luxury fashion brands use — think any printed magazine masthead.

Page Sections Breakdown

Navigation Bar

Logo left, category links centre (Men, Women, Child, Collection, Trends), search field and Login/Sign up pill button right. Minimal, flat, no background — lets the hero breathe.

Hero Section

Two-column asymmetric layout. Left: italic headline + subtext + "Shop now" pill CTA. Right: large image placeholder with a distinctive single-corner-rounded shape — the brand's visual signature.

New Collection Grid

Three category cards — Summer Wear, Ethnics, Western Wear — each with a tonal colour block and bottom label. "See more" CTA below. Section subtitle: "Empowering lives through style and compassion."

Brand Story + Stats

Right-aligned text block with italic headline "Best fashion brand since 2015." Left: large organic-shaped image. Below: three stat pills — 2015 / 9800+ Product sales / 5200+ Best reward.

Best Seller Products

Left column: section title + description + "Learn more" CTA. Right: 2×3 product card grid with tonal placeholder images and "product name" labels at the bottom of each card.

Testimonials Strip

Three full-width testimonial cards with avatar circle and customer name at the bottom. Dark muted background colour differentiates this section from the rest of the page.

Footer

Dark background. Navigation repeated horizontally. Copyright line. Instagram handle: @Vintage_Vogue_. Minimal and clean — no unnecessary clutter.

06

Challenges as a Fresher

No Design System to Reference

As a first-timer, there was no component library or pre-set style guide to lean on. Every decision — font size, card padding, border-radius — had to be made from scratch by eye.

Building Brand Identity from Zero

The brand name, tagline, colour story, and tone of voice were all invented. Creating a coherent brand persona without a brief is harder than designing to one.

Hierarchy Without Colour Contrast

A monochromatic earthy palette means all hierarchy has to come from type size, weight, and spacing alone — colour can't do the work. Getting this right took multiple iterations.

Designing for a Grid Without Knowing Grid Systems

The product grid and collection cards were designed intuitively before any formal knowledge of 8-point grids or column structures — the consistency came from careful eye-matching.

07

Outcomes

Competed Successfully as a Fresher

Submitted a complete, polished landing page design to a college competition with no prior formal design education — proof that instinct and aesthetic sensitivity matter as much as technical training at the start.

Established a Coherent Brand World

Vintage Vogue feels like a real brand — the palette, typography, naming, tagline, and stats all tell a consistent story. For a first project, creating that cohesion is the hardest achievement.

All Key E-commerce Sections Covered

The page includes every section a real fashion landing page needs — hero, collection showcase, brand credibility, product grid, social proof, and footer navigation. Nothing was missing.

Strong Visual Identity Through Restraint

Choosing not to use bright colours, gradients, or decorative elements was a bold call for a first project. The restraint itself became the design's personality — and it paid off.

09

Conclusion

Vintage Vogue is the project that started everything. It's imperfect by every professional standard I now know — no grid system, no accessibility audit, no research, no mobile screens. But it has something that took instinct rather than training: a point of view.

The earthy palette, the italic serif voice, the single-corner-rounded shapes, the quiet restraint — none of those were accidents. They were choices made under time pressure by someone who trusted their eye before they had the vocabulary to explain why. That instinct is what design education refines, not what it creates.

Looking back, this project is less a demonstration of skill and more a demonstration of potential — which is exactly what a first college competition project should be.
